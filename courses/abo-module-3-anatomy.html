Eye Anatomy

The eye has three layers: outer (cornea/sclera), middle (uvea: iris, ciliary body, choroid), and inner (retina). Light passes through cornea, aqueous humor, pupil, lens, vitreous humor to reach the retina.

Key InsightThe cornea provides 2/3 of the eye's refractive power. The lens provides 1/3 and can change shape for focusing.

Refractive Errors

Myopia: eye too long, image focuses in front of retina, corrected with minus lenses. Hyperopia: eye too short, focuses behind retina, corrected with plus. Astigmatism: irregular cornea. Presbyopia: age-related near vision loss.

Key InsightPresbyopia typically begins around age 40 and requires reading addition (Add power).

Common Conditions

Cataracts: clouding of lens. Glaucoma: increased eye pressure damaging optic nerve. Macular degeneration: central vision loss. Diabetic retinopathy: blood vessel damage.

Key InsightCataracts are treated with surgery. Glaucoma damage is irreversible - early detection critical.

Visual Acuity

Visual acuity measures clarity. 20/20 is normal - seeing at 20ft what normal eye sees at 20ft. 20/40 means seeing at 20ft what normal sees at 40ft (worse). Legal blindness: 20/200 or worse.

Key InsightSnellen fractions: numerator is test distance, denominator is distance at which normal eye sees that letter size.

Accommodation

Accommodation: eye's ability to focus near by changing lens shape. Ciliary muscle contracts, lens becomes more convex. Amplitude decreases with age (presbyopia).

Key InsightAmplitude: ~14D at age 10, ~8D at age 30, ~1D at age 60. This is why reading glasses are needed with age.
