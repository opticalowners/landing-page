Patient Education on Safety Factors of Eyewear

The FDA mandates that all prescription lenses must meet minimum impact resistance standards. Understanding these requirements is critical for proper patient counseling and lens material selection.

FDA Drop Ball Test

  • Standard test: 5/8" (16mm) steel ball dropped from 50 inches onto lens
  • Applies to: All dress eyewear lenses
  • Minimum thickness: 2.0mm at thinnest point for standard glass
  • Lens must not: Fracture, chip, or have pieces dislodge

ANSI Z87.1 High-Velocity Impact Test

  • Test method: 1/4" steel ball at 150 ft/sec
  • Required for: Industrial safety eyewear, sports protection
  • Polycarbonate: Only material that passes without additional thickness
  • Trivex: Also meets high-velocity standards
Clinical Pearl

Polycarbonate remains the gold standard for pediatric patients, monocular patients, and those in high-risk occupations. Its impact resistance is 10x greater than standard CR-39 plastic.

Lens Material Safety Profiles

Material Index Impact Resistance Best For
CR-39 Plastic 1.498 Moderate (passes drop ball) Standard dress wear, low Rx
Polycarbonate 1.586 Excellent (10x CR-39) Children, safety, sports, monocular
Trivex 1.532 Excellent Rimless frames, superior optics
High-Index 1.67 1.67 Good High myopia, cosmetic concern
High-Index 1.74 1.74 Moderate Very high myopia
Red Flag

Never dispense glass lenses to children under 18 or for any sports/safety application. Glass lenses, even when heat-treated, shatter into sharp fragments upon impact.

Special Patient Populations

Monocular Patients: MUST have polycarbonate or Trivex for the functional eye. Document informed consent if patient declines.

Pediatric Patients: Polycarbonate is standard of care for children under 18. Consider spring hinges for durability and scratch-resistant coating (poly scratches easily).

Industrial/Occupational: OSHA requires ANSI Z87.1 certified safety eyewear. Look for Z87+ marking on frame and lenses.

UV Protection

  • Polycarbonate: Blocks 100% UV inherently
  • Trivex: Blocks 100% UV inherently
  • CR-39: Requires UV coating to block fully
  • High-Index: Most block significant UV, but verify
üìù Exam Tip

Questions often test when polycarbonate is REQUIRED vs. recommended. Required: children, monocular patients, industrial safety. Recommended: sports, active lifestyles.

Spectacle Lens Designs & Optical Materials

Single Vision Lens Designs

  • Spherical: Single power throughout, traditional design
  • Aspheric: Flatter, thinner profile; reduces edge thickness in plus powers
  • Atoric: Aspheric on both surfaces; optimizes cylinder correction
  • Digital/Freeform: Point-by-point optimization for aberration control
Clinical Pearl

Aspheric lenses are most beneficial for hyperopes (+3.00D and higher) where they significantly reduce the "bug-eye" magnification effect and improve cosmetics.

Prismatic Correction

Prism Fundamentals

Prism bends light toward its base while the image appears displaced toward the apex.

  • Prism diopter (Œî): 1Œî displaces light 1cm at 1 meter
  • Base direction: Where the thickest part of the prism points
  • Prentice's Rule: Œî = F √ó d (where d is decentration in cm)
Clinical Pearl

Remember "BILD" - Base In for exo deviations (eso position), Base Out for eso deviations. The prism base moves the eye in the opposite direction.

Horizontal Deviations

Condition Prism Direction Notes
Esophoria/tropia Base OUT (BO) Split between eyes
Exophoria/tropia Base IN (BI) Split between eyes
Convergence insufficiency Base IN (BI) Often relieving prism only

Vertical Deviations

  • Right hypertropia: BD OD or BU OS
  • Left hypertropia: BD OS or BU OD
  • Rule: Base DOWN in front of the HIGHER eye
üìù Exam Tip

For vertical prism, always place Base DOWN in front of the HIGHER eye. Think "push the high eye down."

Œî = F √ó d where F = lens power in diopters, d = decentration in cm

  • Plus lenses: prism base toward decentration direction
  • Minus lenses: prism base opposite decentration direction

Example: A -5.00D lens decentered 6mm (0.6cm) induces: 5.00 √ó 0.6 = 3.0Œî

Slab-Off

  • Indicated when vertical imbalance exceeds 1.5Œî in reading position
  • Placed on the MORE MINUS (less plus) lens
  • Neutralizes Base Up effect of more minus lens in downgaze

Systematic troubleshooting requires understanding the relationship between symptoms and their potential causes:

Clinical Pearl

For PAL non-adapts, check fitting height FIRST. If the fitting cross is below the pupil center, the patient is looking through the intermediate or near zone for distance‚Äîthe #1 cause of progressive lens failure.

Frame Fitting Problems

  • Nose pads too narrow: Glasses sit too high, marks on nose, discomfort
  • Nose pads too wide: Glasses sit too low, looking over the frame
  • Temples too long: Frame slides forward, sits too low
  • Temples too short: Pressure behind ears, discomfort
  • Excessive pantoscopic tilt: Induced vertical prism, image displacement
  • Insufficient pantoscopic tilt: Poor PAL performance, aberrations
  • Excessive face-form (wrap): Peripheral distortion, induced prism

Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

  1. Verify the prescription: Lensometer check of dispensed glasses vs. written Rx
  2. Check frame fit: PD, OC heights, pantoscopic tilt, vertex distance
  3. Measure current glasses: Compare to previous successful Rx
  4. Review the refraction: Was it consistent? Cycloplegic if needed?
  5. Compare to previous Rx: Large changes may need gradual transition
  6. Allow appropriate adaptation: 1-2 weeks for new Rx, 2-4 weeks for first PAL
  7. Re-refract if necessary: Over-refract over new lenses

Adaptation Expectations

  • New single vision Rx: 1-2 weeks typical adaptation
  • First progressive lenses: 2-4 weeks; may take longer in elderly
  • Large Rx change (>1.00D): May need intermediate step
  • Significant axis change: Often most difficult to adapt
  • Prism (new): Horizontal adapts better than vertical

Patient Education on Risk Factors & Lifestyle Options

The Myopia Epidemic

Myopia prevalence has increased dramatically worldwide, representing a significant public health concern. Understanding risk factors enables targeted prevention and early intervention strategies.

Global Prevalence Statistics

  • Current global prevalence: ~30% of world population myopic
  • Projected 2050: ~50% of world population (5 billion people)
  • High myopia (>-6.00D): Expected to affect 1 billion by 2050
  • East Asia: 80-90% prevalence in young urban adults (Taiwan, Singapore, South Korea)
  • United States: Prevalence doubled from 25% to 42% between 1971-2004
Clinical Pearl

Earlier onset of myopia predicts higher final myopia. A child who becomes myopic at age 6 is far more likely to develop high myopia (-6.00D or worse) than one who becomes myopic at age 12. This makes early intervention critical.

Genetic Risk Factors

Family history is one of the strongest predictors of myopia development:

Parental Myopia Child's Risk Approximate Chance
Two myopic parents 6x increased risk 30-40% will become myopic
One myopic parent 3x increased risk 20-25% will become myopic
No myopic parents Baseline risk ~10% will become myopic

Myopia is polygenic‚Äîover 200 genetic loci have been identified. However, the rapid increase in prevalence over 1-2 generations indicates environmental factors are the primary drivers of the current epidemic.

Environmental Risk Factors

Factor Effect on Myopia Evidence Strength
Near work (reading, screens) Increased risk Moderate
Outdoor time Protective Strong
Education level/years of schooling Higher education = more risk Strong
Urban vs. rural living Urban = higher risk Moderate
Screen time Likely increased risk Emerging

The Outdoor Time Effect

Multiple large studies have demonstrated that time spent outdoors is protective against myopia onset:

  • Sydney Myopia Study (Rose et al.): Children spending >2 hours/day outdoors had 4x lower risk of myopia onset
  • Guangzhou Study (He et al.): Adding 40 minutes of outdoor time during school reduced myopia onset by 23%
  • Taiwan Program: 80-minute outdoor recess reduced myopia from 48% to 8.4%

Mechanism of Outdoor Protection

  • Light intensity: Bright light (>1000 lux) appears protective; outdoor light is 10,000-100,000 lux vs. indoor 100-500 lux
  • Dopamine release: Bright light stimulates retinal dopamine, which inhibits axial elongation
  • Distance viewing: Relaxed accommodation when viewing distant objects
  • Spectral composition: Natural light spectrum may play a role
üìù Exam Tip

Recommend at least 2 hours of outdoor time daily for children. It's the LIGHT EXPOSURE that matters most, not the specific outdoor activity. Even being outdoors in shade provides more light than indoors.

Patient Counseling Points

When counseling parents about myopia prevention and control:

  • Outdoor time: Minimum 2 hours daily; can be cumulative throughout the day
  • 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes of near work, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds
  • Reading distance: Maintain at least Harmon distance (elbow to knuckle) for near tasks
  • Adequate lighting: Ensure proper illumination for near work
  • Limit continuous near work: Take breaks every 30-45 minutes
  • Regular eye exams: Monitor for progression, especially in high-risk children
Red Flag

High myopia (>-6.00D) significantly increases lifetime risk of sight-threatening complications: retinal detachment (5-40x risk), myopic maculopathy, glaucoma (2-3x risk), and early cataracts. Aggressive management should begin early in rapidly progressing children.

Dual-Focus Contact Lens Designs for Myopia Control

Mechanism of Action

Dual-focus (multifocal) contact lenses for myopia control work by creating simultaneous competing focal points on the retina. The underlying theory involves manipulation of peripheral defocus.

Peripheral Defocus Theory

  • Standard single vision correction: Creates peripheral hyperopic defocus in myopic eyes (image focuses behind peripheral retina)
  • Peripheral hyperopia signal: May stimulate continued axial elongation as the eye "grows toward" the focal point
  • Myopia control lenses: Create peripheral MYOPIC defocus (image in front of peripheral retina)
  • Result: Myopic defocus signals the eye to slow or stop axial growth
Clinical Pearl

The center-distance design provides clear central vision for distance while the peripheral treatment zones create myopic defocus to slow eye growth. Think of it as "tricking" the eye into thinking it's already fully grown.

FDA-Approved Dual-Focus Lenses

MiSight 1 Day (CooperVision)

  • Design: Center-distance with concentric ActivControl treatment zones
  • Material: Omafilcon A (daily disposable hydrogel)
  • Dk/t: 28
  • Water content: 60%
  • Age indication: 8-12 years at initiation (can continue beyond)
  • Efficacy: 59% reduction in myopia progression over 3 years
  • Axial length: 52% reduction in axial elongation
  • FDA status: First FDA-approved myopia control contact lens (November 2019)
  • Power range: -0.25 to -10.00D (0.25 steps)

Abiliti 1-Day (Johnson & Johnson Vision)

  • Design: EDOF (Extended Depth of Focus) with RingBoost technology
  • Material: Etafilcon A (daily disposable hydrogel)
  • Age indication: 7-12 years at initiation
  • Efficacy: Similar reduction rates to MiSight in clinical trials
  • FDA approval: 2024

üìù Exam Tip

Fitting Considerations

  • Standard CL evaluation: K readings, HVID, tear film assessment, corneal health
  • Motivation assessment: Both child AND parents must be committed
  • Handling ability: Verify child can insert/remove independently
  • Visual expectations: VA may be slightly reduced vs. single vision (typically 1-2 lines)
  • Potential complaints: Mild halos or reduced contrast initially
  • Follow-up schedule: 1 week, 1 month, then every 6 months minimum

Patient Selection Criteria

Ideal Candidates

  • Ages 8-12 at initiation (can continue wearing into teens/adulthood)
  • Progressive myopia (>0.50D/year progression documented)
  • Motivated child with involved parents
  • Good hygiene habits and anticipated compliance
  • No contraindications to contact lens wear
  • Myopia in treatment range (-0.25 to -10.00D for MiSight)

Contraindications

  • Active ocular infection or inflammation
  • Severe dry eye disease
  • Poor anticipated hygiene or compliance
  • Known allergy to lens materials or solutions
  • Conditions that contraindicate CL wear (severe corneal disease, etc.)
Red Flag

Daily disposable modality is critical for pediatric myopia control‚Äîit maximizes safety and compliance. Do not substitute reusable lenses for FDA-approved daily disposable myopia control designs.

Orthokeratology & Other Contact Lens Designs

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K) Overview

Orthokeratology uses specially designed rigid gas permeable (RGP) lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape the cornea, providing clear daytime vision without correction while simultaneously slowing myopia progression.

Mechanism of Action

  • Reverse geometry design: Lens has a flatter central curve than the cornea, creating hydraulic pressure
  • Epithelial redistribution: Central epithelium thins, mid-peripheral epithelium thickens
  • NOT stromal change: Effect is in epithelium only‚Äîcompletely reversible
  • Peripheral myopic defocus: Mid-peripheral steepening creates myopic defocus similar to soft multifocals
  • Temporary effect: Cornea returns to original shape if lens wear discontinued
Clinical Pearl

Ortho-K effectiveness for myopia control ranges from 36-56% reduction in axial length progression across multiple studies. It's particularly effective for lower to moderate myopia (-1.00 to -4.00D) and works well in children as young as 6-7 years old.

Ortho-K Lens Design Zones

Zone Location Function Design Feature
Base Curve (BC) Central Flattens central cornea Flatter than flat K by treatment amount
Reverse Curve (RC) Para-central Creates tear reservoir Steeper than BC (holds tears)
Alignment Curve (AC) Mid-peripheral Lens centration & stability Aligned to mid-peripheral cornea
Peripheral Curve (PC) Edge Edge lift for tear exchange Flatter for adequate lift

Ortho-K Advantages

  • Lens-free daytime vision: No glasses or contacts during waking hours
  • Reversible treatment: Can discontinue if desired
  • Sports/swimming: Ideal for active children
  • Proven myopia control: Substantial evidence base
  • Early intervention: Can start in children as young as 6-7
  • Parental supervision: Wear at home under parent oversight

Ortho-K Risks and Limitations

  • Microbial keratitis: Higher risk than daily soft lenses (overnight wear)
  • Treatment range: Typically limited to -6.00D myopia, -1.75D astigmatism
  • Corneal staining: Common initially, usually transient
  • Strict compliance: Requires meticulous hygiene and lens care
  • Higher cost: More expensive than standard contact lenses
  • Adaptation period: May take 1-2 weeks for stable vision
Red Flag

Ortho-K carries increased risk of microbial keratitis, especially Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Acanthamoeba. Emphasize strict hygiene: NO tap water exposure, replace cases every 1-3 months, never exceed prescribed wearing time, and seek immediate care for red eye or pain.

  • No contact lens handling or infection risk

Pharmaceutical Management of Myopia

Atropine for Myopia Control

Low-dose atropine has emerged as one of the most effective pharmaceutical interventions for slowing myopia progression, particularly in children. It is now widely used globally, often in combination with optical treatments.

Mechanism of Action

The exact mechanism is not fully understood, but current evidence suggests:

  • Not cycloplegia: Effect appears independent of accommodation at low doses
  • Muscarinic receptors: May act on retinal and/or scleral M1-M4 receptors
  • Scleral remodeling: May inhibit scleral growth and extracellular matrix changes
  • Dopamine pathway: Possible role in modulating retinal dopamine release
  • Nitric oxide: May influence nitric oxide signaling in myopia development
Clinical Pearl

Low-dose atropine (0.01-0.05%) provides good efficacy with minimal side effects and rebound. The optimal concentration based on LAMP and other studies is approximately 0.05%, balancing efficacy against tolerability.

ATOM Studies Summary

The Atropine for Treatment of Myopia (ATOM) studies established the dose-response relationship:

Concentration Efficacy Side Effects Rebound Effect
1.0% ~77% reduction Significant (mydriasis, photophobia, blur) Significant rebound on cessation
0.5% ~75% reduction Moderate Moderate rebound
0.1% ~68% reduction Minimal Mild rebound
0.05% ~67% reduction (LAMP) Minimal Minimal rebound
0.01% ~50% reduction Minimal Minimal rebound

Practical Prescribing

Formulation

  • Commercial availability: Limited; often requires compounding pharmacy
  • Typical concentrations: 0.01%, 0.025%, 0.05%
  • Preservative-free: Preferred for long-term use
  • Storage: Refrigerate compounded preparations; check expiration

Dosing Regimen

  • Frequency: 1 drop at bedtime, nightly (both eyes)
  • Duration: Continue through active myopia progression period (typically until late teens)
  • Starting dose: Often start with 0.01% and increase if needed
  • Monitoring: Check axial length and refraction every 6 months
üìù Exam Tip

Higher concentration atropine = more effective BUT more side effects AND more rebound when stopped. The "sweet spot" is 0.01-0.05% for balancing efficacy with tolerability and minimal rebound.

Red Flag

Avoid atropine in patients with angle-closure risk or narrow angles. Use caution in patients with Down syndrome (increased atropine sensitivity) or light-colored irides (more mydriasis). Always check for drug allergies.

Combination Therapy

Evidence increasingly supports combining treatments for enhanced efficacy in aggressive progressors:

  • Atropine + Ortho-K: Additive effect demonstrated; reduces progression more than either alone
  • Atropine + Soft multifocal CL: Emerging evidence supportive
  • Atropine + DIMS/Stellest spectacles: Being studied; logical combination

Treatment Algorithm Considerations

  1. Step 1: Lifestyle modification‚Äîincrease outdoor time to ‚â•2 hours/day
  2. Step 2: Add optical intervention (ortho-K, dual-focus CL, or DIMS/Stellest spectacles)
  3. Step 3: Consider adding low-dose atropine if progression continues ‚â•0.50D/year
  4. Step 4: Combination therapy (optical + pharmaceutical) for aggressive progressors

Other Pharmaceutical Agents

Several other agents are being investigated but are not yet standard of care:

  • Pirenzepine: M1-selective antimuscarinic; showed efficacy but development discontinued
  • 7-methylxanthine: Adenosine antagonist; limited human data
  • Topical dopamine agonists: Preclinical research stage

Low Vision Definition

Low vision is defined as visual impairment that cannot be fully corrected with standard glasses, contact lenses, medication, or surgery, yet still allows some usable vision. It represents a spectrum between normal vision and total blindness.

WHO Visual Impairment Classifications

Category Visual Acuity (Better Eye, BCVA) Functional Impact
Mild VI 20/30 - 20/60 Difficulty with fine tasks
Moderate VI 20/70 - 20/160 Difficulty reading standard print
Severe VI 20/200 - 20/400 Cannot read newspaper headlines
Profound VI 20/500 - 20/1000 Relies on non-visual cues
Near-total blindness Worse than 20/1000 or VF <10¬∞ Light perception tasks only
Total blindness NLP (no light perception) No visual function
Clinical Pearl

Legal blindness in the US is defined as BCVA ‚â§20/200 in the better eye OR visual field ‚â§20¬∞ in the widest diameter. This definition is important for disability determinations, tax benefits, and access to services‚Äîbut it does NOT mean the patient has no usable vision.

Essential History Components

Ocular History

  • Primary diagnosis: What condition is causing the vision loss?
  • Onset and progression: Sudden vs. gradual; stable vs. progressive
  • Previous treatments: Surgeries, injections, laser treatments
  • Current eye medications: Compliance, side effects
  • Family history: Hereditary conditions (RP, macular dystrophy)
  • Prognosis: Expected course of the condition

Functional Vision Assessment

Ask about specific tasks rather than general "how is your vision?"

  • Reading tasks: Newspaper, books, mail, medication labels, menus, phone
  • Distance tasks: TV, recognizing faces, street signs, bus numbers
  • Mobility: Navigation indoors/outdoors, stairs, curbs, obstacles
  • ADLs: Cooking, grooming, medication management, writing checks
  • Lighting needs: Photophobia vs. need for extra illumination
  • Contrast issues: Problems in dim light or with low contrast
  • Glare sensitivity: Difficulty with bright lights or sunlight

Psychosocial Assessment

  • Living situation: Alone vs. with family/caregiver; accessibility of home
  • Employment status: Working, retired, or seeking employment
  • Vocational needs: Job tasks that require vision
  • Educational goals: School-age patients, continuing education
  • Depression screening: Very common with vision loss (up to 30%)
  • Support system: Family involvement, transportation access
  • Patient goals: What do they WANT to be able to do?
üìù Exam Tip

In low vision, focus on FUNCTION not just acuity numbers. The most important question: "What do you want to be able to do that you can't do now?" This guides device selection and rehabilitation goals.

Specific Testing to Assess Visual Status

Low Vision Acuity Testing

Standard Snellen charts are often inadequate for low vision patients because they don't measure below 20/200 and have unequal letter counts per line. Specialized charts and techniques provide more accurate and clinically useful measurements.

Chart Options

Chart Type Features Best Use
ETDRS Logarithmic progression, equal letters/line, standardized Gold standard for clinical trials and LV
Feinbloom Large numbers 20/200-20/1400 range Severe vision loss quantification
LEA Symbols House, circle, square, apple symbols Children, non-literate patients
Bailey-Lovie LogMAR design, equal difficulty letters Research, precise measurements
Counting Fingers/HM/LP Gross visual function When charts fail
Clinical Pearl

Record low vision acuity in M notation or logMAR for consistency and clinical utility. M notation relates directly to print size: 1M = newspaper print at 40cm. If patient reads 2M at 20cm, they need 4M print at 40cm (standard reading distance).

Contrast Sensitivity Testing

Many low vision patients have reduced contrast sensitivity disproportionate to their acuity loss. This significantly impacts real-world function more than acuity alone.

Testing Methods

  • Pelli-Robson chart: Letters of decreasing contrast (same size); most common clinical test
  • Mars Letter Contrast Test: Similar to Pelli-Robson; portable, hand-held
  • CSV-1000: Sine-wave gratings at different spatial frequencies
  • Functional contrast: Observe patient with low-contrast real-world tasks

Clinical Significance of Contrast Sensitivity

  • Predicts functional vision better than acuity alone
  • Impacts reading speed, mobility, face recognition
  • May be severely reduced even with moderate acuity loss
  • Guides lighting and contrast enhancement recommendations
  • Normal CS: log 1.65-2.0; functional impairment:

Calculating Magnification Needs

The goal magnification is based on the ratio of current acuity to target acuity for the desired task.

Magnification Formula

Required Magnification = Current Acuity Denominator / Goal Acuity Denominator

  • Example: Patient has 20/200 vision, goal is 20/40 for reading newspaper
  • Calculation: 200 √∑ 40 = 5x magnification needed
  • This helps determine device power needed

Types of Magnification

Method How It Works Example
Relative distance Move object closer 40cm to 20cm = 2x magnification
Relative size Use larger print/object 18pt to 36pt font = 2x
Angular magnification Optical device (telescope, magnifier) 4x magnifier provides 4x
Electronic magnification CCTV, tablet zoom Variable, up to 70x+
Red Flag

Contrast sensitivity loss often explains why a patient with "adequate" acuity (e.g., 20/60) cannot function well. Always test contrast sensitivity in low vision patients‚Äîit may be the limiting factor, not acuity.

Non-Surgical Treatment Options for Low Vision

High Addition Spectacle Lenses

Strong reading additions provide relative distance magnification by allowing closer working distances while maintaining clear focus. The formula: Working Distance (m) = 1/Add Power (D).

Add Power Working Distance Equivalent Magnification
+2.50D (standard) 40cm (16") 1x (reference)
+4.00D 25cm (10") 1.6x
+6.00D 17cm (7") 2.4x
+8.00D 12.5cm (5") 3.2x
+10.00D 10cm (4") 4x
+12.00D 8.3cm (3.3") 4.8x
Clinical Pearl

For high adds (>+4.00D), use single vision microscopic readers rather than bifocals or progressives. The very short working distance makes bifocal head postures impractical, and progressives have too narrow a reading zone for these powers.

Optical Magnifying Devices

Hand Magnifiers

  • Advantages: Portable, intuitive, familiar to patients
  • Powers available: Typically 2x to 20x
  • Illuminated versions: Improve contrast and visibility
  • Best for: Spot reading tasks (price tags, labels, menus)
  • Limitations: Hand fatigue with sustained use, need steady hands

Stand Magnifiers

  • Advantages: Fixed focus distance‚Äîno accommodation needed; stable for tremor
  • Design: Preset distance from lens to page
  • Use with spectacles: Often designed to be used WITH reading glasses
  • Powers available: 2x to 20x
  • Best for: Patients with hand tremor, arthritis, sustained reading

Spectacle-Mounted Magnifiers

  • Microscopic spectacles: High plus adds (discussed above)
  • Telemicroscopes: Telescope with reading cap for intermediate distances
  • Prism half-eyes: BI prism allows binocular use at close range
  • Advantages: Hands-free operation
  • Limitations: Cosmesis, narrow field of view, fixed working distance

Telescopes

  • Galilean: Shorter, lighter, upright image, narrower field
  • Keplerian: Longer, wider field, inverted image (needs prism)
  • Uses: Distance tasks (TV, classroom, theater, faces, signs)
  • Bioptic telescopes: Mounted in spectacles for driving (state laws vary)

Electronic Magnification

CCTV/Video Magnifiers (Desktop)

  • Magnification: Variable, typically 2x to 70x+
  • Contrast options: Reverse polarity (white on black often preferred)
  • Working distance: Natural, ergonomic position
  • Features: Line markers, adjustable colors, freeze frame
  • Cost: $500-$3,500+ depending on features
  • Best for: Extended reading, writing, crafts

Portable Electronic Magnifiers

  • Handheld devices: Small camera-based magnifiers
  • Tablet/smartphone apps: Built-in cameras with zoom
  • Advantages: Portable, often already owned, many free apps
üìù Exam Tip

Key advantage of CCTV over optical magnifiers: REVERSE POLARITY (contrast reversal). Many AMD and RP patients see dramatically better with white text on black background. Optical magnifiers cannot provide this.

Lighting and Contrast Enhancement

  • Task lighting: 2-3x ambient illumination for reading tasks
  • Light positioning: Behind and to the side to avoid glare and shadows
  • Recommended levels: 75-150 foot-candles for reading (vs. 30-50 normal)
  • Gooseneck lamps: Allow precise positioning
  • LED vs. incandescent: LED cooler, more efficient; consider color temperature

Contrast Enhancement Strategies

  • High-contrast materials (black pen on white paper, bold-lined paper)
  • Yellow acetate overlays (may improve contrast for some)
  • Absorptive lenses/filters (yellow, amber, plum) for specific conditions
  • Reduce glare with matte surfaces, anti-reflective coatings
  • Good contrast in environment (contrasting edges on stairs, etc.)
Red Flag

For macular degeneration patients, standard lighting recommendations may not apply. Many need MUCH brighter task lighting (75-150 fc) but are also photophobic outdoors. Use directed task lighting rather than overhead ambient light, and recommend quality sunglasses with side shields outdoors.

Clinical Pearl

State Vocational Rehabilitation (VR) agencies provide FREE services for working-age adults with vision loss, including job training, assistive technology, workplace accommodations, and job placement services. This is an underutilized resource‚Äîrefer all working-age patients.

Eccentric Viewing Training

For patients with central scotomas (primarily AMD), training to use a preferred retinal locus (PRL) can significantly improve reading and visual function.

Principles of Eccentric Viewing

  • Identify PRL: Find the best area of remaining functional retina
  • Location: Usually 2-15¬∞ from former fovea
  • Direction: Most common is above or to the left of scotoma
  • Training goal: Consistent, automatic use of PRL for viewing
  • Improvement: Reading speed and accuracy increase with practice

Training Methods

  • Clock face technique: Patient looks at specific "hour" position relative to target
  • Steady eye strategy: Keep eyes still, move reading material
  • Target tracking exercises: Following moving targets with PRL
  • Reading practice: Structured reading exercises with specific gaze direction
  • Microperimetry biofeedback: Advanced real-time feedback (specialized centers)

Orientation and Mobility (O&M) Services

When to Refer for O&M

  • Visual field less than 20¬∞ diameter (tunnel vision)
  • Difficulty navigating, especially in unfamiliar environments
  • History of falls or near-misses
  • Loss of confidence in traveling independently
  • New or recent significant vision loss affecting mobility
  • Patient avoids going out due to vision concerns

O&M Services Include

  • White cane training: Proper techniques for detecting obstacles, drop-offs, stairs
  • Sighted guide technique: Safe travel with human assistance
  • Environmental awareness: Using landmarks, sun position, sounds
  • Public transportation: Bus, train, ride-share services
  • Indoor orientation: Home, workplace, public buildings
  • Street crossing: Traffic patterns, crosswalk timing
üìù Exam Tip

Know the referral triggers: O&M for mobility/safety concerns (VF <20¬∞, falls), OT for daily living skills (cooking, grooming), vocational rehab for employment needs, and mental health for adjustment to vision loss.
