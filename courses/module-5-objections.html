Module 5: Handling Objections

Master the art of turning customer hesitation into confidence. Learn proven techniques to address concerns with empathy and skill, moving customers toward a positive decision.

Understanding Objections

Objections aren't roadblocks—they're road signs. When a customer raises an objection, they're telling you exactly what's standing between them and a purchase. This is valuable information that, handled well, brings you closer to a sale.

Why Objections Are Actually Good

Many salespeople fear objections, but they're actually positive signals:

  • Engagement: An objecting customer is still talking to you
  • Interest: They wouldn't object if they didn't care
  • Information: They're telling you what they need to hear
  • Opportunity: Each handled objection builds trust

The opposite of an objection isn't agreement—it's indifference. A customer who says "I'll think about it" and leaves is far harder to help than one who says "That's more than I wanted to spend."

Key Insight An objection is often a buying signal in disguise. "It's too expensive" often means "Help me understand why it's worth the price." They're asking you to convince them.

Types of Objections

Real objections: Genuine concerns that need to be addressed

  • "I'm not sure my insurance covers this"
  • "I need to check with my spouse first"
  • "I'm worried about adapting to progressives"

Smokescreen objections: Excuses hiding the real concern

  • "I need to think about it" (often means price or uncertainty)
  • "I'm just looking" (might mean they feel pressured)
  • "I'll come back later" (usually means they won't)

The Right Mindset

How you view objections shapes how you handle them:

  • Don't take it personally: They're objecting to the situation, not you
  • Stay curious: Seek to understand before responding
  • Be patient: Rushing creates more resistance
  • Stay confident: Your belief in your product matters
Pro Tip: Welcome objections with phrases like "I'm glad you brought that up" or "That's a great question." This signals that you're not defensive and creates space for dialogue.

The LAER Framework

LAER is a proven four-step framework for handling objections effectively. It ensures you fully understand the concern before responding, which dramatically improves your success rate.

L - Listen

Let the customer fully express their concern without interruption:

  • Give them your complete attention
  • Don't start formulating your response while they're talking
  • Use body language that shows you're engaged
  • Let them finish completely—they may reveal the real issue

A - Acknowledge

Show that you've heard and understand their concern:

  • "I understand your concern about the price."
  • "That's a completely valid question."
  • "I hear you—adapting to new lenses can be a worry."
  • "Many customers feel the same way initially."

Acknowledgment isn't agreement—it's validation that their concern matters.

Key Insight Skipping the Listen and Acknowledge steps is the biggest mistake in objection handling. When customers don't feel heard, they dig in deeper. Take time to understand before you respond.

E - Explore

Ask questions to fully understand the objection:

  • "Can you tell me more about what's concerning you?"
  • "When you say it's too expensive, what are you comparing it to?"
  • "What would need to be different for this to work for you?"
  • "Is it the total price or the out-of-pocket that's the issue?"

Exploring uncovers whether this is a real objection or a smokescreen, and gives you the information needed to respond effectively.

R - Respond

Now—and only now—address the objection:

  • Tailor your response to what you learned
  • Use specific benefits that address their concern
  • Provide evidence or social proof when relevant
  • Check if your response addressed their concern

After responding, confirm: "Does that help address your concern?" or "How does that sound?"

Pro Tip: Practice LAER until it becomes automatic. Most salespeople jump straight to "Respond" and wonder why their objection handling fails. The first three steps are where the magic happens.

Price Objections

Price is the most common objection in sales—and the most misunderstood. When customers say something is "too expensive," they're rarely saying they can't afford it. They're saying they don't yet see the value.

What "Too Expensive" Really Means

Price objections usually translate to one of these:

  • "I don't understand why it costs this much"
  • "I'm not convinced it's worth it for me"
  • "I've seen similar things for less elsewhere"
  • "This is more than I budgeted"
  • "I'm afraid of making the wrong decision"

Each of these needs a different response. Use the Explore step to find out which one you're dealing with.

Key Insight Never defend price—build value instead. If you act like the price needs defending, you're agreeing it's too high. Confidently reinforce the value they're getting.

Strategies for Price Objections

Reframe the value:

  • "This isn't just eyewear—it's clear, comfortable vision for the next two years"
  • "Think of it as an investment in your eye health and daily comfort"
  • "Quality eyewear affects everything you see, all day, every day"

Break down the cost:

  • "Over two years, that's less than $1 per day"
  • "Compared to what you spend on coffee each month, this is..."
  • "Your insurance covers $X, so your investment is really only..."

Compare alternatives:

  • "The cheaper option would mean thicker lenses and no anti-glare—would that work for your job?"
  • "We could reduce the price by removing the blue light protection, but you mentioned computer eye strain..."

Address hidden concerns:

  • "Is it the total amount, or the out-of-pocket today?"
  • "Would spreading the payment help make this work?"
  • "What were you expecting to invest in eyewear today?"

What NOT to Do

  • Don't immediately discount: This devalues your product and your recommendation
  • Don't be defensive: Avoid "Well, quality costs money"
  • Don't badmouth cheaper alternatives: Compare fairly and professionally
  • Don't assume they can't afford it: It's about perceived value, not wealth
Pro Tip: If you've built value properly during presentation, price objections are much easier to handle. Prevention is better than cure—build value early.

Common Optical Objections

Beyond price, optical customers have specific concerns related to eyewear. Being prepared with confident responses to these common objections will help you serve customers better and close more sales.

"I can get it cheaper online"

Listen & Acknowledge: "I understand—online prices can look attractive."

Explore: "What's drawing you to buy online? Is it purely price, or convenience?"

Respond:

  • Emphasize what they get here: professional fitting, adjustments, warranty service
  • "When you buy online, who adjusts them if they don't fit right?"
  • "Our price includes fitting, follow-up adjustments, and local warranty service"
  • "Online measurements are often less accurate, leading to vision problems"

"I had trouble with progressives before"

Listen & Acknowledge: "I'm sorry you had that experience—adaptation issues are frustrating."

Explore: "Can you tell me what happened? What specifically gave you trouble?"

Respond:

  • "Progressive technology has improved dramatically in recent years"
  • "The issue may have been the fitting or the lens design—let me show you what's different now"
  • "We offer an adaptation guarantee—if you can't adjust, we'll work with you until you can"
Key Insight Past negative experiences create strong objections. Show empathy first, then explain what's different now. Never dismiss their previous experience as their fault.

"I need to check with my spouse"

Listen & Acknowledge: "Absolutely, it's smart to make decisions together."

Explore: "What information would help them make the decision with you?"

Respond:

  • Offer to call or FaceTime the spouse
  • Take photos of them in the frames to share
  • Provide a detailed quote they can take home
  • "Would it help if I wrote down everything we discussed for you to share?"

"I'll wait until my insurance renews"

Listen & Acknowledge: "That makes sense—insurance benefits are valuable."

Explore: "When does your plan renew? Are you having any issues with your current eyewear?"

Respond:

  • If they have unused current benefits: "Did you know your current benefits expire and don't roll over?"
  • If they're struggling now: "Would it be worth investing now for months of better vision?"
  • "We can file with your new insurance once it's active—let's get you set up now"

"These frames don't look right on me"

Listen & Acknowledge: "Finding the right look is important—you'll wear these every day."

Explore: "What specifically isn't working? The shape, size, color?"

Respond:

  • Offer alternatives based on their feedback
  • "Let me show you a few styles that might suit your face shape better"
  • "Sometimes it takes a few tries—that's completely normal"
Pro Tip: Keep a mental list of your most common objections and practice your LAER responses until they feel natural. Preparation builds confidence.

When to Let Go

Not every objection can be overcome, and not every customer will buy. Knowing when to gracefully step back is as important as knowing how to handle objections. Pushing too hard damages relationships and your reputation.

Signs It's Time to Let Go

Recognize when continued effort won't help:

  • Repeated objections: They keep raising the same concern despite your responses
  • Disengagement: Short answers, checking phone, looking away
  • Genuine constraints: Real budget limits or timing issues you can't solve
  • Emotional escalation: Frustration or irritation is building
  • Multiple smokescreens: New objections keep appearing after you address each one
Key Insight A graceful exit preserves the relationship. Today's "no" can become tomorrow's "yes" if you leave on good terms. Pushing until they're frustrated ensures they never return.

How to Let Go Gracefully

Acknowledge their position:

  • "I understand this isn't the right time"
  • "It sounds like you need more time to decide, and that's completely fine"
  • "I want you to feel confident in whatever you decide"

Leave the door open:

  • "When you're ready, I'd love to help you find the perfect eyewear"
  • "Here's my card—feel free to reach out with any questions"
  • "Would it be okay if I followed up with you in a few weeks?"

Provide value anyway:

  • Give them helpful information to take home
  • Answer any final questions they have
  • Make a genuine recommendation even if they don't buy today

The Follow-Up Opportunity

Many sales happen after the first interaction:

  • Get permission to follow up: "May I give you a call in a couple weeks?"
  • Capture their contact information
  • Note what they were interested in and their concerns
  • Set a reminder to reach out at an appropriate time
  • Follow up with value, not pressure: "I found some information on progressive adaptation that might help..."

Learning from "Lost" Sales

Every objection you can't overcome is a learning opportunity:

  • What could you have done differently?
  • Did you miss something during discovery?
  • Was there a value gap you didn't address?
  • How can you prevent this objection next time?
Pro Tip: The way you handle "no" matters more than you think. Customers remember how you made them feel. A respectful exit often leads to referrals and future business.
