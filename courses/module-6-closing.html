Module 6: Closing

Learn to recognize when customers are ready to buy and confidently guide them to a decision. Master closing techniques that feel natural and create satisfied customers.

The Psychology of Closing

Closing isn't about tricks or manipulation—it's about helping customers make a decision they'll feel good about. Understanding the psychology behind buying decisions helps you close more sales while building trust and satisfaction.

Why Customers Need Help Deciding

Even when customers want to buy, several psychological factors can prevent them from taking action:

  • Decision fatigue: Too many choices lead to paralysis
  • Fear of regret: Worry about making the wrong choice
  • Loss aversion: Pain of spending money feels immediate; benefits feel distant
  • Need for validation: Wanting confirmation they're making a good choice
  • Procrastination: Defaulting to "later" when "now" feels hard

Your job as a closer is to help customers overcome these barriers—not by pressuring them, but by making the decision easier and more comfortable.

Key Insight The best closers don't "close" customers—they help customers close themselves. When you've done the earlier steps well, closing feels like a natural conclusion, not a high-pressure moment.

The Mindset Shift

Many salespeople fear closing because they see it as pushy. Reframe your thinking:

  • Old mindset: "I'm trying to get them to buy"
  • New mindset: "I'm helping them get what they came for"

If you've qualified properly and presented genuine solutions to real needs, asking for the sale is a service, not a sales tactic.

When Closing Goes Wrong

Closing feels pushy when:

  • You haven't built enough value first
  • You're closing on your timeline, not theirs
  • You're using manipulation instead of consultation
  • You ignore signals that they're not ready
  • You make it about your needs, not theirs
Pro Tip: If you dread closing, you probably didn't build enough value earlier. Go back and strengthen your presentation skills—strong value makes closing easy.

Recognizing Buying Signals

Timing is everything in closing. Close too early and you seem pushy; close too late and you miss the moment. Learning to recognize buying signals helps you know exactly when to move toward a decision.

Verbal Buying Signals

Listen for language that indicates readiness:

  • Ownership language: "When I wear these..." "My new glasses will..."
  • Future visualization: "I can see myself wearing these to work"
  • Logistics questions: "How long until they're ready?" "Do you deliver?"
  • Payment questions: "Can I use my HSA?" "What's my insurance cover?"
  • Commitment signals: "I think I've decided" "This is the one"
  • Seeking reassurance: "These really are good quality, right?"
Key Insight When customers start talking about the purchase as if it's already happened ("When I wear these..."), they've mentally bought. Your job is to make it official.

Non-Verbal Buying Signals

Watch body language for readiness cues:

  • Extended handling: Reluctance to put frames down
  • Multiple mirror checks: Repeatedly looking at themselves
  • Nodding: Agreement while you talk about benefits
  • Relaxed posture: Comfort and openness
  • Leaning forward: Engagement and interest
  • Reaching for wallet/phone: Preparing to transact

Context Signals

Situational cues that suggest readiness:

  • They've narrowed down to one or two options
  • They've addressed all their concerns through questions
  • They've mentioned a timeline or event
  • They've already invested significant time with you
  • They've involved a family member in the decision

Reading the Moment

Buying signals often cluster together. When you see multiple signals at once, it's time to close:

  • They're wearing the frames and won't take them off
  • They're asking about turnaround time
  • They're nodding and smiling
  • This combination = green light to close
Pro Tip: When you see a buying signal, test it with a closing question. If they respond positively, proceed. If not, address any remaining concerns and watch for the next signal.

Closing Techniques

While the best closes feel natural, having a toolkit of techniques gives you flexibility to match your approach to different customers and situations. Here are proven methods that work in optical retail.

The Assumptive Close

Proceed as if they've already decided to buy:

  • "Let me get your measurements so we can order these"
  • "Which case would you like with these—the hard shell or the soft pouch?"
  • "I'll start processing your insurance while you look at cases"

Best for: Customers who've shown clear buying signals and just need a gentle push forward.

The Summary Close

Recap the value before asking for commitment:

  • "So we have the lightweight titanium frames you loved, with progressive lenses that will work for both computer and driving, plus the blue light protection for your long work days. Ready to move forward?"

Best for: Complex purchases where reminding them of all the benefits reinforces the decision.

Key Insight The summary close works because it combats loss aversion. By listing everything they're getting, you shift focus from what they're spending to what they're gaining.

The Alternative Close

Give them a choice between two "yes" options:

  • "Would you like the anti-reflective coating or the premium anti-reflective with the scratch warranty?"
  • "Do you want to pick these up, or should we ship them to your home?"
  • "Would you prefer to use your insurance now or save it for later in the year?"

Best for: Customers who struggle with decisions and need the choice simplified.

The Urgency Close

Highlight genuine time-sensitive factors:

  • "Your insurance benefits expire at the end of the month—let's make sure you use them"
  • "This frame is limited edition and we only have two left"
  • "If we order today, you'll have them before your trip"

Best for: Customers who procrastinate. Only use genuine urgency—never fabricate it.

The Question Close

Ask a question that leads to commitment:

  • "Is there anything else you need to know before we get started?"
  • "Do you have any remaining concerns, or are we ready to move forward?"
  • "What would you need to see to feel confident about this decision today?"

Best for: Uncovering hidden objections or confirming readiness.

Pro Tip: Don't memorize scripts—understand the principles. The best closes sound like natural conversation, not rehearsed techniques.

Asking for the Sale

Many salespeople do everything right—build rapport, qualify, present value—and then never actually ask for the sale. The most important closing skill is simply having the confidence to ask.

Why Salespeople Don't Ask

Common fears that hold people back:

  • Fear of rejection: "What if they say no?"
  • Fear of being pushy: "I don't want to pressure them"
  • Fear of ruining rapport: "We were having such a nice conversation"
  • Assuming they'll ask: "If they want it, they'll tell me"

Here's the truth: customers expect you to ask. When you don't, they often feel uncertain and leave without buying—even if they wanted to.

Key Insight Studies show that 60% of customers say "no" four times before saying "yes." Most salespeople give up after just one or two attempts. Polite persistence pays off.

Simple Ways to Ask

Asking doesn't have to be complicated:

  • "Ready to go ahead with these?"
  • "Should we get started on your order?"
  • "Would you like to take these home today?"
  • "Can I ring these up for you?"
  • "Let's get you set up with these."

The Power of Silence

After you ask, stop talking:

  • The first person to speak after the close often "loses"
  • Silence gives them space to process and decide
  • Filling silence with more talking can undo your close
  • Count to 10 in your head if you need to

Handling "Not Yet"

If they're not ready, don't panic:

  • Ask what's holding them back (uncover the real objection)
  • Address the concern using LAER from Module 5
  • Try a different closing approach
  • If they're genuinely not ready, gracefully step back
Pro Tip: Practice asking for the sale out loud. The words should feel comfortable and natural. If they feel awkward, you'll hesitate when it matters.

After the Close

The sale isn't over when they say "yes." What happens after the close determines whether you've made a one-time transaction or gained a loyal customer who refers others. The post-close experience is crucial.

Confirm and Reassure

Immediately after they commit:

  • Congratulate their decision: "Great choice—you're going to love these"
  • Reduce buyer's remorse: "These are one of our most popular frames for good reason"
  • Confirm details: Review what they're getting to ensure clarity
  • Thank them sincerely: "I really appreciate you trusting us with your eyewear"
Key Insight Buyer's remorse peaks immediately after purchase. Proactive reassurance at this moment prevents cancellations and returns while building loyalty.

Set Clear Expectations

Prevent future disappointments by being clear:

  • Timeline: "Your glasses will be ready in 7-10 days"
  • Process: "We'll call you when they arrive, then schedule a fitting"
  • Adaptation: "Progressives may take a few days to adjust to—that's normal"
  • Care: "Here's how to clean and store them properly"
  • Warranty: "You're covered for scratches for the first year"

Plant Seeds for the Future

Build the relationship beyond this sale:

  • Mention add-ons: "When you're ready, we have prescription sunglasses that work great with your lifestyle"
  • Schedule follow-up: "Let's book your adjustment appointment now"
  • Encourage reviews: "If you love them, we'd appreciate a Google review"
  • Ask for referrals: "We love referrals—if you know anyone who needs eyewear..."

The Handoff

If someone else handles checkout or dispensing:

  • Introduce the customer by name
  • Summarize what they purchased and why
  • Share any personal details to maintain connection
  • Don't disappear—stay available for questions

Follow Up

After they leave:

  • Send a thank-you message (email, text, or handwritten note)
  • Check in after they've had the glasses for a week
  • Address any issues quickly and generously
  • Remember them when they return
Pro Tip: The sale you make today is easier than finding a new customer tomorrow. Invest in the post-close experience—it's where referrals and repeat business come from.
