The Economics of Loyalty

Understanding the financial impact of loyal patients transforms how you approach every interaction.

Calculating Patient Lifetime Value (PLV)

PLV = Average Annual Spend × Years as Patient × Referral Factor

Example: $450 × 12 years × 1.3 = $7,020

Key InsightWhen you see a patient, you're looking at a potential $7,000+ relationship—not a $450 transaction. This changes how you handle problems.

The Compound Effect

Small retention improvements create massive results. A 5% improvement (80% to 85%) over 5 years means 116 more long-term patients—potentially $800,000+ in lifetime value!

The Referral Multiplier

  • Satisfied patients tell 4-6 people
  • Referred patients have 16% higher lifetime value
  • Referred patients have 37% higher retention
  • 92% trust referrals from people they know

Cost of Poor Retention

  • Lost direct revenue — future purchases elsewhere
  • Lost referrals — they won't recommend you
  • Negative word-of-mouth — unhappy patients tell 9-15 people
  • Replacement cost — marketing to find new patients
Pro Tip: Post your PLV where staff can see it. When everyone knows a patient is worth $7,000, a $50 adjustment becomes an obvious investment.

Retention vs. Acquisition

Most practices spend the majority of their budget on acquisition. But over-focusing on new patients while neglecting retention is like filling a leaky bucket.

The Leaky Bucket Problem

  • Add 100 new patients, lose 80 existing = net growth of 20
  • Add 50 new patients, lose only 20 = net growth of 30
  • Second scenario costs less and builds stability
Key InsightFixing the leak (retention) is often more cost-effective than pouring faster (acquisition). Yet most practices spend 80% on acquisition, only 20% on retention.

When to Focus on Each

Prioritize Acquisition: New practice, expanded capacity, new market, retention already 85%+

Prioritize Retention: Patient base shrinking, getting complaints, low return rates, declining referrals

Healthy Balance

  • 15-25% of patients should be new each year
  • 80%+ of existing patients should return
  • 30%+ of new patients from referrals
Pro Tip: Track both metrics monthly. If returning rate drops below 75%, pause acquisition and focus on understanding why.

The Patient Journey

Retention isn't a single moment—it's shaped by every interaction across the entire journey.

Five Stages

1. Awareness — Discover your practice (search, referral, advertising)

2. Consideration — Evaluate you (reviews, website, phone call)

3. Experience — The visit (exam, selection, checkout)

4. Post-Purchase — After leaving (wearing glasses, adjustments)

5. Loyalty — Ongoing relationship (recalls, communication)

Key InsightMost practices focus heavily on Stage 3 but neglect Stages 4 and 5. Yet post-purchase and loyalty phases are where long-term retention is built.

Mapping Touchpoints

  • Pre-visit: Website, phone, reminder, parking
  • Check-in: Greeting, paperwork, wait time
  • Exam: Tech interaction, doctor, explanation
  • Optical: Frame selection, recommendations
  • Checkout: Payment, pickup scheduling
  • Post-visit: Thank you, pickup, adjustments
  • Between visits: Recalls, birthday messages
Pro Tip: Mystery shop your own practice. Have a friend go through the entire journey and give honest feedback.

Building a Retention Culture

Retention isn't just a strategy—it's a culture. When everyone thinks "retention first," it becomes automatic.

What It Looks Like

  • Everyone understands patient lifetime value
  • Problems are opportunities to strengthen relationships
  • Staff empowered to make things right
  • Follow-up is systematic, not optional
  • Patient satisfaction is measured regularly
  • "Would this make them want to come back?" is constant
Key InsightCulture comes from leadership. If the owner treats retention as priority, staff will too. If leadership only talks about new patient numbers, that's what staff will focus on.

Creating the Shift

1. Share the why: Educate on PLV, share retention stats

2. Empower your team: Authority to resolve issues, celebrate retention wins

3. Measure what matters: Track return rates, satisfaction scores, referrals

4. Systematize: Standard follow-up procedures, retention in job descriptions

Daily Habits

  • Morning: Review yesterday's patients for follow-ups
  • Each interaction: Use name, eye contact, smile
  • Problems: Own them, solve them, confirm resolution
  • Checkout: Schedule next appointment before they leave
Pro Tip: Start each week with a "retention win" story. Share examples of staff who went above and beyond. What gets celebrated gets repeated.
