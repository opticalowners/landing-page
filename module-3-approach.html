Module 3: Approach & Engagement

Master the art of making first contact and building genuine rapport with customers. Learn techniques that create trust and set the stage for successful sales conversations.

The First Impression

You never get a second chance to make a first impression. In optical retail, those first few seconds when a customer walks through your door set the tone for the entire interaction—and potentially a long-term relationship.

The 7-Second Rule

Research shows that people form their first impression within 7 seconds of meeting someone. In that brief window, customers are subconsciously evaluating:

  • Trustworthiness: Can I rely on this person?
  • Competence: Do they know what they're doing?
  • Warmth: Do they care about me?
  • Professionalism: Is this a quality establishment?
Key Insight First impressions are sticky—once formed, they're difficult to change. A positive first impression creates a halo effect where customers interpret subsequent interactions more favorably.

Elements of a Strong First Impression

Your appearance:

  • Clean, professional attire appropriate for your practice
  • Well-groomed and presentable
  • Wearing quality eyewear (you're in the optical business!)
  • Name badge visible and readable

Your body language:

  • Stand up straight with open posture
  • Make eye contact and smile genuinely
  • Move toward the customer (don't wait for them to come to you)
  • Keep hands visible and gestures welcoming

The environment:

  • Clean, organized displays
  • Good lighting and comfortable temperature
  • Pleasant background music at appropriate volume
  • Clear pathways and accessible products
Pro Tip: Acknowledge every customer within 10 seconds of entry, even if you're busy. A simple smile and "I'll be right with you" shows they matter.

Opening Lines That Work

The words you choose in your first interaction matter. A great opening line breaks the ice, puts the customer at ease, and opens the door to genuine conversation—without feeling scripted or pushy.

Avoid the Classic Mistakes

Some opening lines are so overused they've become ineffective:

  • "Can I help you?" — Too easy to dismiss with "Just looking"
  • "Let me know if you need anything" — Passive and forgettable
  • "Are you finding everything okay?" — Assumes browsing, not buying
  • "What brings you in today?" — Can feel interrogative too early
Key Insight The best opening lines are specific, observational, and invite conversation rather than a yes/no response. They show you're paying attention and genuinely interested.

Opening Lines That Create Connection

Observational openers:

  • "Those frames you're looking at are one of our most popular styles this season."
  • "I noticed you're drawn to the blue tones—great choice for your coloring."
  • "That's our new titanium collection—incredibly lightweight."

Warm welcomes:

  • "Welcome! Have you visited us before, or is this your first time?"
  • "Good afternoon! Beautiful day to be shopping for new eyewear."
  • "Hi there! I'm [Name]. Feel free to explore, and I'm here when you're ready."

For returning customers:

  • "Great to see you again! How are those glasses working out?"
  • "Welcome back! I remember you—you got the Ray-Bans last spring, right?"

The Power of Using Names

If you can identify returning customers or check them in by name:

  • Use their name naturally in your greeting
  • Reference their previous purchase or preference
  • This personal touch dramatically increases connection
Pro Tip: Match your energy to the customer's. If they seem rushed, be efficient. If they're relaxed and browsing, take a more leisurely approach.

Building Rapport

Rapport is the foundation of trust. When customers feel a genuine connection with you, they're more open, more honest about their needs, and more likely to buy—and return. Building rapport isn't manipulation; it's authentic human connection.

The Science of Rapport

Rapport happens when people feel understood and valued. It's built through:

  • Mirroring: Subtly matching body language, pace, and tone
  • Common ground: Finding shared interests or experiences
  • Active listening: Showing genuine interest in what they say
  • Empathy: Understanding and acknowledging their feelings
Key Insight People buy from people they like and trust. Investing time in rapport early actually shortens the overall sales process because customers become more open and decisive.

Rapport-Building Techniques

Find common ground:

  • Notice what they're wearing, carrying, or talking about
  • "I love that bag—is that from the new collection?"
  • "I see you're wearing running shoes—do you run competitively?"
  • Connect their interests to eyewear needs naturally

Use active listening:

  • Maintain appropriate eye contact
  • Nod and use verbal acknowledgments ("I see," "That makes sense")
  • Paraphrase what they've said to confirm understanding
  • Ask follow-up questions that show you were listening

Show genuine interest:

  • Ask about their lifestyle, not just their prescription
  • Remember details they share and reference them later
  • Share appropriate personal information to create reciprocity

Rapport Killers to Avoid

  • Interrupting or finishing their sentences
  • Looking at your phone or appearing distracted
  • Pushing products before understanding needs
  • Using jargon that makes them feel uninformed
  • Being too familiar too fast (respect boundaries)
Pro Tip: The best rapport feels effortless. If you're trying too hard, step back and focus on genuine curiosity about the person in front of you.

Reading the Customer

Every customer is different. Some want hands-on help from the moment they walk in; others prefer to browse independently. Learning to read customer cues helps you provide the right level of engagement at the right time.

Customer Personality Types

The Browser:

  • Wants space to explore independently
  • May feel overwhelmed by too much attention
  • Approach: Greet warmly, offer availability, then step back
  • Check in periodically without hovering

The Researcher:

  • Has done homework and knows what they want
  • Values expertise and detailed information
  • Approach: Respect their knowledge, add value with insider insights
  • Don't oversimplify or seem patronizing

The Decisive Buyer:

  • Knows what they need and wants efficiency
  • Appreciates directness and quick service
  • Approach: Get to the point, streamline the process
  • Minimize small talk unless they initiate

The Uncertain Customer:

  • Needs guidance and reassurance
  • May feel overwhelmed by choices
  • Approach: Ask questions, narrow options, provide recommendations
  • Be patient and supportive
Key Insight Watch, listen, and adapt. The customer's behavior tells you how they want to be treated. Your job is to match their preferred style, not force yours on them.

Reading Body Language Cues

Open and engaged:

  • Facing you directly, making eye contact
  • Leaning in, nodding along
  • Handling products, asking questions
  • Signal: They're ready for more engagement

Closed or hesitant:

  • Avoiding eye contact, turning away
  • Crossed arms, stepping back
  • Short answers, checking phone
  • Signal: Give them space or address a concern
Pro Tip: When in doubt, ask: "Would you like me to walk you through some options, or would you prefer to browse on your own first?" Let them choose their experience.

Transitioning to Discovery

Once you've made a positive first impression and built initial rapport, it's time to transition into the discovery phase—learning about the customer's specific needs so you can provide the best solution. This transition should feel natural, not abrupt.

Signs They're Ready for Discovery

Look for cues that indicate the customer is comfortable and ready to engage more deeply:

  • They're asking questions about products or services
  • They've mentioned a specific need or problem
  • They're making eye contact and seem engaged
  • They've stopped browsing and focused attention on you
  • They've shared something personal (job, lifestyle, concerns)
Key Insight The transition to discovery should feel like a natural progression of the conversation, not a shift into "sales mode." The customer shouldn't feel like a switch was flipped.

Smooth Transition Phrases

From browsing to discovery:

  • "So I can point you in the right direction, tell me a bit about how you use your current glasses."
  • "To make sure we find the perfect fit, can I ask a few questions about your lifestyle?"
  • "I'd love to help you narrow down the options—what's most important to you in new eyewear?"

From small talk to discovery:

  • "You mentioned you work at a computer all day—have you noticed any eye strain?"
  • "Since you said you're into cycling, have you considered prescription sports eyewear?"
  • "It sounds like your lifestyle is pretty active—let's find frames that can keep up!"

Setting Expectations

Help customers understand what comes next:

  • "I'll ask a few questions to understand your needs, then show you some options I think you'll love."
  • "Let's spend a few minutes figuring out exactly what you need—then we can explore the best solutions."
  • "My goal is to find you eyewear that you'll absolutely love wearing every day."

The Bridge to Presentation

Your discovery questions should naturally lead to your product presentation:

  • Each answer gives you information to tailor your recommendations
  • By the end of discovery, you should know their needs, budget, and timeline
  • This makes your presentation feel like a customized solution, not a generic pitch
Pro Tip: Think of discovery as a conversation, not an interrogation. Weave questions naturally throughout your interaction rather than firing them off in sequence.
